Environmental Justice Addresses the Disproportionate Effects of Air Pollution on Black and Latino Communities

Óscar Sanchez, an environmental activist running for alderman of Chicago’s 10th Ward, grew up watching his family and friends suffer the negative health consequences from the city’s high levels of air pollution.

“My brother had to wear a mask to breathe from 5 to 10 years old,” Sanchez said. “My family has high blood pressure … if they're exposed to bad quality air, they have the possibility of having strokes. Air quality is not only something that causes respiratory issues, it is also an aggressor to other conditions.”

Sanchez explained that he grew up seeing more of his friends through visits at the hospital rather than being able to play outside with them. He said this caused him to question why his community that was both Latino and poor experienced this more than neighboring communities.

According to the Chicago Department of Public Health, air pollution is the leading cause of Chicago’s nine-year life expectancy gap between Black and white residents a nd the decreases in life expectancy in the Latino population that Sanchez saw throughout his childhood.

“We can say that people of color are still exposed to the highest concentration [of air pollution],” said Sara Camilleri, the postdoctoral scholar who led a study investigating higher rates of premature death from nitrogen dioxide exposure for minority groups. “When we look at the health impacts, this result is further amplified in the sense that we see even larger portions of people of color living in areas that are experiencing health burdens related to NO2.”

This study, conducted by the Northwestern University Climate Change Research Group, found that 171,000 premature deaths per year in the United States are linked to nitrogen dioxide exposure, and in predominantly Black census tracts, this rate of premature deaths was 47% higher than the national average.

“You see a high concentration of industry in the south and west sides of the city, which are predominantly Black and Latino,” said José Acosta-Cordova, Senior Transportation Policy Analyst for the Little Village Environmental Justice Organization. “We are, in fact, the largest industrial hub in North America. We have more land dedicated for heavy industry than any other city in the United States.”

The location of industry hubs were largely caused by the legacies of racist housing policies from the 1930s developed and enforced by the federal government. However, the location of these industries were not the only factors increasing these negative health outcomes.

“It's a little complicated because it's not just where is the air pollution the worst,” said Brian Urbaszewski, Director of Environmental Health Programs of the Respiratory Health Association in Chicago. “It's because of those underlying other challenges of poverty, racism, lack of access to consistent medical care, other stressors, like crime in the community, that are all contributing to that factor.”
