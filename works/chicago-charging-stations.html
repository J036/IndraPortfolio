The Gap

A 14% progress rate with 9 years left

When Chicago released its 2022 Climate Action Plan, the city committed to enabling 2,500 new public passenger EV charging stations by 2035. At the time, the Inflation Reduction Act was about to pass, electric vehicle adoption was accelerating nationally, and the goal seemed ambitious but achievable.

As of March 27, 2026, the city has 350 publicly available electric charging locations, counting every Level 1, Level 2, and DC Fast Charger on the Chicago Data Portal database. That is 14% of the target, with roughly 9 years remaining.

Progress toward 2,500-station goal 350 / 2,500
14% complete · 2,150 stations short · Deadline: 2035

To hit the target on time, Chicago would need to add approximately 239 new charging locations every year from now through 2035. In the six years since the CAP was drafted, the city has averaged far less than that.

"Chicago has a lower number of chargers per vehicle than the country, state, and County."

— CDOT Chicago Moves Electric Framework, April 2025 draft, p.31
Historical Growth

EV registrations outpaced charger buildout

Chicago's EV registration growth has been remarkable with a 10× increase from 1,865 registered battery-electric vehicles in 2018 to 20,328 by October 2024, according to the Illinois Secretary of State's office. However, the charging network has not kept pace.

The divergence between the two lines tells the underlying story: as more Chicagoans buy electric vehicles, partly driven by federal tax credits that no longer exist, the infrastructure meant to support them has grown at a fraction of the pace.

Despite having the highest share of registered EVs among the geographies compared, Chicago has the worst charger-to-vehicle ratio, 1 charger for every 47 EVs, versus the national average of 1 for every 25.

Equity Gap

7.8% of chargers are in environmental justice neighborhoods

The distribution problem is not just one of quantity. Only 7.8% of the city's charging stations sit in EJ-designated neighborhoods, the communities that bear the heaviest burden of air pollution and have the most to gain from EV infrastructure.

The highest concentration of EV chargers remains in downtown Chicago and the North Side. The South and West Sides, where residents are most likely to rely on street parking and where air quality is worst, have the fewest options.

Federal Policy Shock

The credits that were supposed to close the gap are gone

The 2023 IRA Addendum to Chicago's CAP estimated that federal programs would help close the charging gap. That analysis is now obsolete. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4 2025, eliminated the primary federal mechanisms supporting EV charging expansion.

Apr 2022
Chicago CAP published. Commits to 2,500 new public EV charging stations by 2035. Federal credits are core to the funding strategy.
Aug 2022
IRA signed. 30C EV charger tax credit activated (up to 30% of installation cost). NEVI Formula Program allocates $148M to Illinois for charging corridors.
Sep 2024
$15M federal grant awarded. USDOT CFI grant approved for Chicago, targeting libraries, Midway Airport, transit hubs.
Jan 2025
EO 14154, funding freeze. Trump administration freezes all IRA and IIJA disbursements. NEVI halted. $15M grant implementation at risk.
Jul 2025
One Big Beautiful Bill signed. Permanently terminates 30C EV charging tax credit (after June 30 2026), $7,500 consumer EV credit (30D), and $4,000 used EV credit (25E).
By the end of 2026
30C credit expires. Final deadline for charger installations to qualify. After this, no federal subsidy for public EV charging infrastructure exists.
2035
CAP deadline. At current pace, Chicago would reach approximately 500–600 locations, roughly 20–24% of the 2,500-station goal.

What Remains

State programs remain, but they're smaller

The loss of federal 30C credits does not mean all funding is gone. Illinois CEJA, ComEd's beneficial electrification programs, and the $15M federal CFI grant provide a floor, but a lower one.

CDOT's own next steps, a curbside charging pilot, Low-Emission Zone proposals, ongoing ComEd engagement, are meaningful but incremental. None substitute for the scale of investment the federal programs would have provided.
